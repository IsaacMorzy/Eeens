  ## The KSH / sqft number is on the listing

  Every industrial listing on this site shows the price per square foot in the right rail, alongside
  the KSH per month for the unit as a whole. We have done this since 2020. The other three formal
  landlords in our corridor don't publish the figure. We get asked about it often enough that the
  answer probably belongs in writing.

  ## The argument for publishing

  Distributors and light-manufacturing operators in Mlolongo and Syokimau generally have a budget per
  square foot in mind before they call. They know what their last lease cost. They know what their
  competitor's lease cost. They know what their cost-of-goods shift tolerance is for the new unit.

  When the per-sqft figure is on the listing, the first ten minutes of the call is the conversation
  that matters: which unit, which loading profile, which lease term. When it is not on the listing,
  the first ten minutes is asking for a number the operator could have looked at in the same place
  they saw the photograph.

  Operators we have on-boarded since 2020 have told us, with one exception, that the per-sqft figure
  is what they care about and what they used to compare listings across brokers. We have lost
  listings because we are not the cheapest. We lose more because we are not on the candidate list at
  all if the operator can't make us comparable in 30 seconds.

  ## The argument against publishing

  A landlord can hold back the per-sqft figure as a negotiating lever. If the listing shows a
  range, or no figure, the operator has to call, and the conversation about the figure happens on
  the landlord's terms.

  We tried this between 2018 and 2019. The conclusion we drew was that lever is small and the
  friction cost is large. An operator who calls without a number is also an operator who will call
  three other landlords; we now find ourselves in a comparison we did not know we were in, on a
  figure we did not show, at the start of a call.

  ## Where publishing makes the conversation harder

  It makes the figure easier to negotiate down by an informed buyer. A buyer who knows the corridor
  median has leverage we would rather not give them on a per-listing basis. The trade-off in our
  book is acceptable: we publish because most operators are informed buyers anyway, and the rest
  benefit from seeing a transparent figure next to a transparent listing.

  It also makes it harder to have a seasonal promotion. We have done two: a reduced deposit in Q3 2024,
  and a free first-month offer on a single Baba Dogo unit in Q1 2026. Both of those are now in the
  figure itself, not a separate "contact us for our introductory pricing" line. We did not like
  writing them. We liked writing the rest of the listing more.

  ## What we will not publish

  Broker fee and deposit amount. The 1-month broker fee and 2-month deposit are policy, not price;
  they are what they are across every listing, and an operator who deals with us once knows them.
  Putting them on the listing invites a comparison we don't want to invite.
