  ## Five figures per industrial listing

  Every warehouse or godown listing on this site shows up to five values on the spec sheet:

  - **Power** — kVA available at the metre
  - **Water** — m³ of municipal supply per day
  - **Parking bays** — count
  - **Floor loading** — kN / m²
  - **Clear height** — metres to the underside of the roof truss

  These come from the surveyor's report that closes on a unit before we publish it. If a unit does not
  have one of these in the report, we do not put a number on the listing. We will not guess.

  ## Power, in kVA

  The figure is what's available at the meter, not at the transformer. If you are running 30 kW of
  freezer compressors and 50 kW of lighting, the meter reading at full load is around 80 kW, which
  converts to roughly 92 kVA including the standard power factor. Add a 20% margin for inrush on the
  compressor start and you are looking at a unit that needs at least 110 kVA at the meter.

  Most of our Mlolongo and Syokimau godowns are 200 kVA to 450 kVA at the meter. Baba Dogo business
  parks run higher, 600 kVA to 1,200 kVA on three-phase.

  If your load profile is mostly resistive (lighting, ICT, small appliances), the kVA you need is roughly
  the kW. If it is mostly inductive (motors, compressors, welding), the kVA you need is roughly 15% to
  20% higher than the kW.

  ## Water, in m³ / day

  This is municipal supply. It is not the borehole yield, it is not the tank capacity, and it is not the
  sum across multiple sources. If we list `120 m³ / day`, you can draw 120 m³ from the municipal meter
  before the supplier restricts you.

  A single production shift on a light-manufacturing line plus hygiene adds up to roughly 8 m³ / day
  per 50 staff. A distribution-only warehouse with no on-site cleaning uses closer to 3 m³ / day.

  ## Parking bays

  Published as a count. The count is what the county-approved site plan allows, not what the current
  tenants happen to be using on the day of the survey. We have had tenants repaint the bays into
  smaller cells to fit cycle docks; the listing still reflects the planned count.

  ## Floor loading, in kN / m²

  This is the slab capacity for distributed load. For palletised goods on standard racking at 2.5
  tonnes per pallet on a 1.0 m × 1.2 m footprint, the distributed load works out to roughly 20 kN / m²
  on the heaviest column. Our steel-roofed warehouses are typically rated to 30 kN / m² or 50 kN / m².
  Anything below that is sold as godown / light-storage, not warehouse.

  ## Clear height, in metres

  Measured to the underside of the lowest roof member. Not to the ridge. Not to the eaves. If the truss
  drops a chord across the bay, that chord counts. We have rejected surveyor's reports that quoted the
  ridge height.

  Six metres gives one level of racking plus a forklift clearance. Eight metres gives two. Ten metres
  gives three plus mezzanine offices.

  ## What's not on the spec sheet

  Truck-court turning radius, dock-leveller count, and racking-supplier history. These matter to
  distribution operators, and we publish them by request. The five figures above are the ones that
  every operator we have worked with has needed within the first hour of looking at a listing, and so
  they go on the listing itself.
